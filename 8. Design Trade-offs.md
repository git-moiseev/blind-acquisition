# 8. Design Trade-offs

The proposed waveform minimizes the computational cost of blind detection by introducing only a small amount of additional signal structure.

This simplification is not obtained without cost.

The waveform design is governed by several competing constraints.

### 8.1 Frequency Separation

The offset $e$ between the nominal carrier frequency and each frequency state is a key waveform design parameter.

A smaller value of $e$ places the two states closer together. This reduces their spectral distinguishability and requires greater frequency resolution from the receiver. Since the receiver architecture and signal-processing capabilities are unknown, excessively small separation may prevent the two states from being resolved during blind acquisition.

A larger value of (e) improves distinguishability but moves the frequency states farther from the nominal carrier. In the presence of an unknown Doppler shift, the observed frequencies are

$$
f_{\pm}=F_0+\Delta f_D\pm e.
$$

If

$$
|\Delta f_D|\le d,
$$

then the complete set of possible observed frequencies extends over

$$
F_0-(d+e)
\le
f_{\pm}
\le
F_0+(d+e).
$$

Therefore, increasing $e$ increases the search margin required around the expected carrier frequency. If the receiver searches only within $F_0\pm d$, one of the frequency states may fall outside the search interval when the Doppler shift approaches its limiting value.

The frequency separation must therefore satisfy two competing requirements:

* it must be large enough for the two states to remain distinguishable by a receiver with unknown frequency resolution;
* it must be small enough that both states remain within a practical search interval under the expected Doppler uncertainty.

No universal optimum follows from the waveform alone. The choice of $e$ depends on the assumed Doppler range, the frequency interval likely to be searched by an unknown receiver, and the minimum receiver resolution for which the waveform is intended to remain detectable.

---

## 8.2 Observation Time and Frequency Stability

Increasing the observation time improves spectral resolution and detection sensitivity.

In practice, however, coherent observation time is limited by

- Doppler drift,
- oscillator instability,
- propagation effects,
- and receiver frequency accuracy.

Consequently, the frequency-state separation cannot be made arbitrarily small.

The optimum value depends on the maximum coherent integration time achievable by the receiver.

---

## 8.3 Packet Duration

Long packet duration improves spectral purity and increases the efficiency of coherent integration.

However, longer packets delay the observation of the complementary packet repetition required for temporal synchronization.

Short packets reduce synchronization latency but increase spectral broadening due to more frequent frequency transitions.

Packet duration therefore represents a trade-off between spectral concentration and synchronization time.

---

## 8.4 Why Constant Power Requires Two Frequency States

Maintaining constant transmitted power while conveying binary information imposes a fundamental constraint on the waveform.

If only a single carrier frequency is available, binary transmission requires periodically switching that carrier on and off. During one binary state the transmitter radiates energy, while during the other it remains silent.

Consequently,

- the transmitted power is no longer constant,
- the signal spends approximately half of its time absent from the channel,
- the receiver loses integration time,
- the waveform acquires an unnecessary amplitude structure.

These characteristics directly contradict the design objective of continuous transmission with constant emitted power.

The smallest modification that preserves both information transfer and continuous transmission is the introduction of a second frequency state.

Instead of switching between transmission and silence, the transmitter continuously radiates while alternating between

$$
F_0-e
$$

and

$$
F_0+e.
$$

Binary information is therefore represented by frequency selection rather than transmitter activity.

At every instant the transmitter radiates the same total power, satisfying the constant-power requirement while preserving continuous observability.

---

## 8.5 Why Two Frequency States Are Sufficient

Once two frequency states are available, the receiver can determine every parameter required for blind acquisition:

- carrier midpoint,
- frequency separation,
- Doppler shift,
- waveform consistency.

Introducing additional frequency states increases signal complexity without providing fundamentally new information.

A third frequency state does not improve Doppler estimation, since the midpoint is already uniquely defined by two frequencies.

Likewise, additional states do not increase robustness against narrowband interference, because any interference affecting one frequency can already be rejected by verifying the expected two-frequency geometry.

More frequency states also increase

- occupied bandwidth,
- transmitter complexity,
- search space,
- receiver processing cost,

while providing no corresponding reduction in acquisition uncertainty.

Therefore, two frequency states represent the minimum complete frequency geometry: sufficient to resolve the ambiguity inherent in a single carrier, yet no more complex than necessary for blind acquisition.

Under the design objective of minimizing receiver computational cost, increasing the number of frequency states beyond two yields diminishing returns without introducing additional information required by the acquisition process.

## 8.6 Why Frequency-State Encoding

Several modulation techniques can convey binary information. However, the design objectives established in this work require simultaneous satisfaction of the following conditions:

- continuous transmission,
- constant emitted power,
- narrow occupied bandwidth,
- computationally efficient blind acquisition,
- robustness against narrowband interference.

No conventional modulation satisfies all of these requirements simultaneously.

#### Amplitude Modulation

Amplitude-based modulation conveys information by varying transmitted power.

As a result,

- the average transmitted power is reduced,
- part of the available transmitter energy carries no information,
- receiver integration efficiency decreases,
- amplitude variations become indistinguishable from propagation-induced fading.

Therefore, amplitude modulation violates the objective of maximizing continuously radiated power.

#### Continuous Frequency Modulation

Continuous frequency modulation preserves transmitted power but distributes signal energy over a substantially wider spectrum.

The resulting increase in occupied bandwidth

- reduces spectral power density,
- enlarges the receiver search space,
- increases computational acquisition cost.

This directly contradicts the objective of minimizing occupied bandwidth.

#### Phase Modulation

Phase modulation preserves both transmitted power and narrow occupied bandwidth.

However, information is encoded in the instantaneous carrier phase.

Blind acquisition therefore requires the receiver to estimate carrier frequency, Doppler shift, and phase simultaneously before information can be extracted.

Unknown propagation delays, oscillator offsets, and phase instability make initial acquisition substantially more computationally demanding than simple spectral detection.

Furthermore, a narrowband interferer occupying the carrier frequency corrupts the entire information stream.

#### Binary Frequency-State Encoding

Encoding information by switching between two narrow frequency states satisfies all design objectives simultaneously.

It provides

- continuous transmission,
- constant emitted power,
- narrow occupied bandwidth,
- immediately observable spectral structure,
- simple blind acquisition based on frequency geometry.

A narrowband interferer can affect at most one of the two transmitted frequencies, while the second frequency state remains available for detection and consistency verification.

Broadband interference affects all modulation schemes equally and therefore does not influence the relative comparison presented here.

Consequently, binary frequency-state encoding represents the simplest modulation strategy that simultaneously satisfies every design requirement established in this work.

## 8.7 General Remarks

The objective of the proposed waveform is not to maximize any individual performance metric.

Instead, the waveform balances bandwidth, observation time, synchronization latency, and computational complexity in order to minimize the overall effort required for blind detection.

The resulting design should therefore be viewed as an engineering compromise rather than an optimum with respect to a single criterion.
